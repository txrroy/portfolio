Solution 1: Rebuilding User Trust & Ease of Use

Heuristic evaluation revealed critical usability issues, while user interviews exposed deeper behavioral patterns:

Design Iteration 1: Radical Simplification

Working with engineering on an elastic search POC using NLP, we experimented with a radical approach:

  • ‚úÖSingle search interface - Google-like simplicity
  • ‚úÖNLP-powered tagging - Automatic service categorization
  • ‚úÖContextual suggestions - Smart recommendations based on input

üö® Critical Learning: Failing Early

Usability testing revealed a fundamental flaw: users didn't want a Google-like search for workplace services. It didn't match their mental model and felt unfamiliar in a corporate context. We learned to meet users where they are, not where we think they should be.

Design Iteration 2: Balanced Innovation

The Pivot Strategy

We restored the familiar service catalog structure but enhanced it with smart shortcuts and improved navigation. This balanced innovation with familiarity.

Key Design Decisions:
  • ‚Ä¢ Maintained department-based navigation
  • ‚Ä¢ Added smart search as secondary option
  • ‚Ä¢ Streamlined form flows by 60%
  • ‚Ä¢ Implemented progressive disclosure

Privacy & Trust Solutions

  • ‚Ä¢ Private tickets route directly to intended department

Reflection & Key Learnings

Critical insights that shaped my approach to complex enterprise design challenges

What This Project Taught Me

ü§ù Co-Creation Over Consultation

True transformation happens when you design with people, not just for them. Our users became co-creators, not just requirement sources. This led to more meaningful and sustainable solutions.

üéØ Meet Users Where They Are

Our radical simplification failed because we didn't match user mental models. The most innovative solution isn't always the most disruptive one - sometimes it's the one that feels familiar while solving problems.

üîÑ Failing Early Saves Time

Our first iteration failure taught us more than any research phase could. Building prototypes quickly and testing with real users prevented months of building the wrong thing.

What I Would Do Differently

Areas for Improvement

Earlier Technical Validation

While our radical simplification taught us valuable lessons about user expectations, we could have validated technical constraints earlier. A deeper technical discovery phase might have guided us toward the balanced solution faster.

More Robust Change Management

Although adoption was successful, we underestimated the training needs for power users. A more comprehensive change management program could have accelerated adoption by 3-6 months.

Accessibility Integration

While we met basic accessibility standards, I would now integrate accessibility research from day one rather than as a compliance check. Enterprise users have diverse needs that benefit from inclusive design thinking.

Metrics Framework

Our success metrics evolved organically. Establishing a comprehensive measurement framework earlier would have provided better insights for iteration and stakeholder communication.

Broader Impact on My Design Practice

This project fundamentally shaped how I approach complex enterprise design challenges:

Stakeholder Dynamics

I learned to navigate complex organizational politics not through avoidance, but through transparency, shared ownership, and incremental wins that build confidence.

Research Methodology

The importance of triangulating qualitative insights with quantitative data became clear. User quotes provide empathy, but usage data provides validation.

Design Leadership

Leading design in large organizations requires balancing user advocacy with business constraints. The best solutions emerge from this tension, not from avoiding it.

System Thinking

Enterprise design is system design. Individual features matter less than how they work together to create coherent, trustworthy experiences at scale.
